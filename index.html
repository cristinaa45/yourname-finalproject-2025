Fashion in Ecuador is more than just clothing—it's a powerful expression of cultural identity. The country's diverse Indigenous communities each have their own styles, shaped by centuries of tradition and environmental influences. Clothing is often handmade using natural fibers and dyes derived from local plants. One standout material is tagua, known as “vegetable ivory,” which symbolizes Ecuador’s creativity and commitment to sustainability.

During the Spanish colonial period, European fashion was introduced and slowly fused with Indigenous styles, giving rise to a rich mestizo tradition. The resulting garments are not only beautiful but tell a story of resistance, resilience, and identity. Today, Ecuadorian fashion continues to evolve, balancing heritage and innovation.

Famous Designers

Olga Fisch: Hungarian-born artist who embraced Ecuador's vibrant Indigenous aesthetics. Her famous rugs blended modern style with local tradition. Olga created job opportunities for artisans and helped Ecuadorian culture reach global audiences through her gallery in Quito.

Galo Sánchez: A visionary designer known for using colorful Andean fabrics in modern clothing. His work revives traditional techniques, making them appealing to younger generations and fashion shows worldwide.

Paulina Anda: Renowned for her elegant handmade shoes and handbags. Paulina empowers local artisans and promotes ethical fashion through her brand ANDA, showcased in Milan and Bogotá.

Traditional Clothing

Poncho: The poncho is a classic Andean garment, worn especially in the Ecuadorian highlands. Woven from wool and dyed with natural colors, its square design is both warm and symbolic. Patterns and colors often represent a person’s community or family roots, blending function with deep cultural meaning.
Manta: Worn by Indigenous women, the manta is a large, often embroidered shawl made of cotton or wool. It is fastened at the front and used for warmth, ceremonies, and daily tasks like carrying babies or goods. Each manta tells a story through its colors and embroidery, linking generations of women across time.
Tagua: Tagua, or vegetable ivory, is a white seed used in jewelry and buttons. Harvested sustainably, it provides eco-friendly income for artisans. Polished and sometimes dyed, tagua is carved into colorful accessories worn in both traditional and modern designs, connecting nature with fashion.
